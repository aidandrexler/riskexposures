Consider three advisors working for the same client. The CPA has structured the client's business income to minimize self-employment taxes, recommending that salary be kept low and income taken primarily as distributions. The financial advisor has built a portfolio of investment accounts titled jointly with the client's spouse. The estate planning attorney has drafted a revocable living trust and recommended that most accounts be retitled into the trust.

Each advisor has given technically defensible advice for their domain. The CPA's recommendation is sound tax planning. The financial advisor's joint titling is efficient for estate planning purposes. The estate attorney's revocable trust recommendation is appropriate for probate avoidance.

Now look at what these three recommendations produce together from an asset protection perspective. The low salary eliminates the federal wage garnishment exemption that would have protected employment income. The joint account titling may have interrupted Tenancy by the Entireties protection that would have made those accounts invisible to a single-spouse creditor. The revocable trust provides zero creditor protection and may have been funded with assets that should have remained in differently structured holding vehicles.

None of these advisors made an error in their domain. The problem is that nobody looked at the picture as a whole.

## Why Silos Form

The silo problem is structural. Each advisor's engagement is scoped to their specific discipline. The CPA's job is tax minimization and compliance. The financial advisor's job is investment management and portfolio construction. The estate attorney's job is wills, trusts, and beneficiary designations. Asset protection — the analysis of how all of these elements interact with creditor exposure — is a separate specialty that does not fit neatly into any of their default scopes.

Unless the client specifically asks one of these advisors to evaluate the full picture, or engages a specialist whose job is to do exactly that, the coordination review does not happen.

## What Coordination Failures Look Like

The most common coordination failures follow predictable patterns.

A tax recommendation that creates personal liability: a business structure optimized for tax purposes that has no charging order protection against personal creditors, meaning a personal judgment from any source gives the creditor direct access to the business equity.

An insurance strategy that has a structural gap: umbrella coverage that seems adequate but is scoped to cover only certain types of claims, combined with a professional liability policy that excludes a category of exposure, combined with no structural layer to address the gap above policy limits.

A trust that contradicts the entity plan: a revocable trust funded with LLC membership interests that removes them from the charging order protection they would have provided in the original owner's name under some structures, or that changes the titling in a way that affects TBE protection on other assets.

An investment account titling that undermines exemption planning: accounts opened as joint tenants rather than Tenancy by the Entireties because the account opening form defaulted to a standard joint ownership checkbox that the financial advisor did not specifically address.

## The Coordination Review

A coordination review is not a comprehensive estate planning engagement or a full asset protection overhaul. It is a structured analysis of how the existing plan elements interact with each other from a creditor exposure and structural coherence perspective. It asks: does the tax structure work with the entity structure? Does the entity structure work with the insurance coverage? Do the account titlings work with the TBE analysis? Does the trust structure work with the asset protection goals?

The output is a gap list — specific inconsistencies, missing elements, and structural conflicts that can be addressed incrementally. For most people who have worked with multiple advisors over many years without a coordination review, the gap list contains at least a few items worth addressing.

## Getting the Advisors in the Room

The practical obstacle to coordination is that each advisor is appropriately focused on their own domain and does not see their job as comprehensive plan review. The coordination function has to be initiated by someone — either the client or an advisor specifically engaged to provide it.

This is one of the core value propositions of a coordinated planning practice: not replacing any of the existing advisors, but functioning as the integrating layer that ensures their independent recommendations do not conflict in ways that create unintended exposure.

The diagnostic tool on this site asks specifically about advisor coordination because the absence of that coordination is consistently one of the highest-value gaps to identify and address.
