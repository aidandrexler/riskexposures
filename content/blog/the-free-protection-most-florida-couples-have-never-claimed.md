Married Florida residents have access to a creditor protection that requires no attorney, no trust, no entity structure, and no ongoing cost. It is available right now by operation of Florida law. Most married couples are not using it — not because they chose not to, but because nobody told them it existed or explained what it requires.

It is called Tenancy by the Entireties, and it is one of the most powerful protections in Florida's creditor exemption framework.

## What Tenancy by the Entireties Does

Under Florida law, when a married couple holds an asset as Tenancy by the Entireties — sometimes abbreviated TBE — that asset is completely exempt from the claims of a creditor holding a judgment against only one spouse. The protection is not partial. It is not reduced. The asset is invisible to a single-spouse creditor entirely.

The Florida Supreme Court confirmed the scope of this protection in *Beal Bank, SSB v. Almand and Associates*, 780 So.2d 45 (Fla. 2001), holding that the TBE presumption applies to personal property — including bank accounts and investment accounts — held jointly by a married couple, not just real estate. This decision significantly expanded the practical reach of TBE protection beyond what many people assume.

## What It Requires

For Tenancy by the Entireties protection to apply, the asset must be held by both spouses jointly, with right of survivorship, as part of an intact marriage. The key word is both — single-spouse creditors cannot reach an asset that is legally owned by the marital unit as a whole.

For real estate, the deed must specifically state Tenancy by the Entireties or use language that clearly establishes that ownership form. A deed stating "joint tenants with right of survivorship" is not the same thing and does not provide TBE protection. This distinction is where most couples go wrong.

For bank accounts and investment accounts, Florida courts apply a presumption under Beal Bank — accounts held jointly by a married couple are presumed to be TBE property absent evidence to the contrary. But this presumption can be overcome, and relying on a presumption rather than explicit documentation creates unnecessary risk. The more conservative approach is to title accounts explicitly as TBE at the financial institution level.

## Why Most Couples Are Not Protected

The gap between who could benefit from TBE titling and who actually has it comes down to one thing: nobody checked. Estate planning attorneys focus on beneficiary designations, trust structures, and probate avoidance. Real estate attorneys focus on the transaction. Financial advisors open accounts however the paperwork defaults to. Nobody in any of these engagements is typically asking: is this titled correctly for TBE purposes?

The result is that most married couples hold their home as joint tenants with right of survivorship — not TBE. Their bank accounts default to whatever language the bank's standard account agreement uses. Their brokerage accounts are titled however the financial advisor's new account form populated.

All of this is correctable. For real estate, it requires a new deed — a relatively simple document that retitles the property in TBE form. For accounts, it requires working with the financial institution to update the titling language.

## The One Creditor TBE Cannot Stop

There is one category of creditor that TBE does not protect against: a creditor with a judgment against both spouses jointly. If a debt is owed by the marital unit — a joint credit card, a jointly signed loan, a guarantee signed by both spouses — TBE titling does not prevent collection from jointly held TBE assets.

This is why TBE planning works best as one layer of a coordinated structure, not as a standalone solution. It eliminates single-spouse creditor exposure on jointly held assets. It does not address joint obligations, inside liability from a business entity, or the full range of professional liability exposure.

## The Practical Starting Point

The first step is knowing what you have. A title review of real property, a review of account titling at each financial institution, and a review of any investment or brokerage accounts will reveal whether TBE protection is actually in place or merely assumed. In most cases the review takes less than an hour and surfaces corrections that take another hour to implement.
