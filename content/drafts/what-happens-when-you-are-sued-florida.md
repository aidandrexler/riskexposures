Most people with significant wealth know they should have asset protection. Far fewer know what actually happens — procedurally, step by step — when a plaintiff's attorney attempts to collect a judgment against them in Florida. Understanding the mechanics clarifies what is actually at risk and why certain structural decisions matter more than others.

## Step One: The Judgment

A lawsuit concludes with either a settlement or a judgment. A judgment is a court order establishing that you owe a specific amount. Once a judgment is entered, it becomes a lien against all real property you own in the county where it is recorded under Fla. Stat. §55.10. The judgment creditor has 20 years to collect — there is no short collection window that makes a judgment go away.

The judgment does not automatically reach all your assets. It is the starting point, not the ending point. What happens next determines what the creditor can actually take.

## Step Two: The Public Records Pull

Within days of a judgment being entered, a plaintiff's attorney will conduct a systematic review of public records. This includes the Florida Division of Corporations to identify your business entities and their structure, the county property appraiser for real estate owned in your name or through entities you control, UCC filings with the Florida Secretary of State for personal property secured interests and evidence of personal guarantees, and court records for prior litigation and judgments.

This review takes a few hours and costs almost nothing. It tells the creditor's attorney everything that is owned, how it is held, and which collection theories are available.

## Step Three: The Writ of Execution

For assets held in your personal name that are not exempt, the collection mechanism is a writ of execution — a court order directing the county sheriff to levy on specific non-exempt personal property. Bank accounts, investment accounts, and personal property are reachable through this process. The process moves quickly once the writ is issued.

## Step Four: The Judgment Lien on Real Property

Real estate you own personally — other than your Florida homestead — is subject to the judgment lien that attached automatically when the judgment was recorded. The creditor can pursue a forced sale of non-exempt real property under Fla. Stat. §56.061. Investment properties, commercial real estate, and land held in your personal name are all reachable.

Your Florida homestead — properly titled, properly exempt — is not reachable by most judgment creditors. The homestead exemption is absolute against most claims, with narrow exceptions for mortgage lenders, tax authorities, and mechanics lienors.

## Step Five: The Entity Investigation

If your assets are held in business entities, the collection process becomes more complex — and this is where structural planning creates or destroys leverage.

For LLC membership interests, the exclusive remedy in Florida under Fla. Stat. §605.0503 is a charging order. The creditor can receive distributions from the LLC if and when distributions are made, but cannot seize the membership interest itself or force the LLC to distribute. A properly structured and maintained LLC creates a genuine collection obstacle.

For S-corporation shares or Professional Association interests, no equivalent protection exists. Those interests are directly attachable — the creditor can become a shareholder with full inspection, accounting, and derivative action rights.

If the creditor can demonstrate commingling or failure to observe corporate formalities, they can pursue veil-piercing — asking the court to disregard the entity entirely and hold you personally liable for entity debts or allow collection of personal assets for entity obligations.

## Step Six: Discovery in Aid of Execution

A judgment creditor in Florida has the right to conduct post-judgment discovery — depositions, document requests, interrogatories — specifically to locate assets. This is called discovery in aid of execution. You can be compelled to appear for deposition and answer questions about every asset you own, every account you hold, every entity in which you have an interest, and every transfer you have made in recent years.

Transfers made after a judgment — or in anticipation of litigation — face fraudulent transfer scrutiny under Fla. Stat. §726.105. The creditor can ask the court to reverse those transfers and return the assets to your personal estate where they are reachable.

## What This Means for Planning

The procedural sequence makes clear why planning before any litigation exists is the only planning that works without legal complications. The homestead exemption requires proper titling and ownership in advance. TBE protection requires correct account and property titling in advance. LLC protection requires proper entity selection, maintenance, and formality documentation in advance. Transfers made after litigation exists — or even after the circumstances that make litigation foreseeable — are presumptively voidable.
