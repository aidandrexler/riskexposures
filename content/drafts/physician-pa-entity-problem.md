When a Florida physician forms a medical practice, the default entity recommendation from most general practice attorneys and accountants is the Professional Association — the PA. It satisfies Florida's professional services licensing requirements, it has a familiar structure, and it has been used for physician practices for decades.

It also provides weaker creditor protection than the alternative — and in a profession with some of the highest personal liability exposure in any field, that gap matters.

## What the PA Actually Is

A Professional Association in Florida is governed by the Florida Professional Service Corporation Act, Fla. Stat. §621. It is a corporate structure — with shares, shareholders, officers, and directors — specifically authorized for licensed professionals including physicians, attorneys, and accountants.

Like any corporation, the PA creates an entity that is legally distinct from its shareholders. The physician-owner is not automatically personally liable for the PA's debts simply by virtue of ownership. That liability barrier for inside claims — debts and obligations of the entity — is real and functions similarly to any other corporate structure.

The problem arises on the outside liability side.

## The Charging Order Gap

Florida's LLC statute, Fla. Stat. §605.0503, provides that the exclusive remedy of a judgment creditor against an LLC member's interest is a charging order. The creditor receives the right to distributions if and when the LLC makes them — but cannot attach the membership interest itself, cannot compel distributions, and cannot exercise any management or voting rights.

This protection is significant. A creditor who holds a charging order against an LLC interest has a paper right that may never produce a payment. The uncertainty of recovery creates negotiating leverage for the debtor.

PA shares carry no equivalent protection. A PA is a corporation, and corporate shares in Florida are personal property that are directly attachable by a judgment creditor. Under Fla. Stat. §56.061, a creditor can obtain a writ of execution against the shares themselves. The creditor becomes a shareholder — with inspection rights, accounting rights, and the right to receive dividends.

In a physician practice context, this means a personal creditor — arising from a car accident, a personal loan default, a divorce proceeding, anything entirely unrelated to the practice — can become a co-owner of the practice with full shareholder rights. That creates immediate practical problems for a practicing physician who cannot easily share ownership with an unknown third party.

## The Structural Alternative

Florida permits physicians to own and operate medical practices through professional limited liability companies — PLLCs — under the Florida Revised LLC Act. A PLLC satisfies the same professional licensing requirements as a PA while providing charging order protection on the ownership interest.

The conversion from a PA to a PLLC involves entity reorganization that requires attention to licensing, credentialing, payer contract assignments, and ongoing compliance obligations. It is not a trivial administrative task. But for a physician with meaningful personal assets outside the practice, the structural protection difference justifies the work.

## The Malpractice Interaction

One important clarification: neither the PA nor the PLLC protects the physician personally from personal malpractice liability. Under Florida law, a licensed professional remains personally liable for their own negligent acts regardless of the entity structure. Fla. Stat. §621.07 preserves this personal liability for professional service corporations.

Malpractice liability flows to the physician personally regardless of entity type. Entity selection matters for outside liability — personal creditors pursuing practice ownership interests — not for malpractice exposure directly. Malpractice exposure is addressed through insurance coverage, practice structure limiting joint liability, and the broader asset protection architecture outside the practice entity.

## The Integrated Picture

For a Florida physician, entity selection is one decision in a set of interconnected decisions. The entity type affects outside liability protection. The entity's maintenance — separate finances, documented formalities, appropriate capitalization — affects inside liability protection and veil-piercing risk. Payer contract structure affects audit and recoupment exposure. Insurance coverage addresses the professional liability layer. And the personal asset structure — homestead, TBE titling, exempt assets, retirement accounts — addresses what is reachable if all other layers are penetrated.

No single decision is sufficient. The PA to PLLC conversion is worth pursuing, but it addresses one dimension of a multi-dimensional exposure profile.
