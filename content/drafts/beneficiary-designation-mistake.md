There is a document sitting on file at your retirement plan administrator and your life insurance company that will determine where a substantial portion of your wealth goes when you die. It overrides your will. It overrides your trust. It overrides whatever instructions your estate planning attorney carefully drafted. And most people have not looked at it in years.

It is your beneficiary designation form. It is one page. It takes thirty minutes to review and update. The consequences of getting it wrong can be irreversible.

## How Beneficiary Designations Work

When you designate a beneficiary on a retirement account — a 401(k), IRA, pension, or profit-sharing plan — or on a life insurance policy, that asset passes directly to the named beneficiary upon your death. It does not go through your estate. It does not pass under your will. It does not follow the instructions in your trust.

This is not a loophole or an oversight in the law. It is by design. These accounts are contractual arrangements between you and the financial institution or insurer, and the beneficiary designation is the controlling document for how those assets transfer.

The Supreme Court confirmed the scope of this principle in *Egelhoff v. Egelhoff*, 532 U.S. 141 (2001), holding that ERISA preempts state law in determining beneficiary rights on employer-sponsored retirement plans. The practical consequence is that a decades-old designation can override a current spouse's claims, a recent will, and everything else — in ways that state courts cannot correct.

## The Most Common Failures

The most frequent beneficiary designation problem is a designation that names a prior spouse. Divorce does not automatically revoke a beneficiary designation on a retirement account or life insurance policy in most circumstances. A person who divorced fifteen years ago, remarried, and updated their estate plan may have inadvertently left a $400,000 IRA to their first spouse because nobody revisited the designation form after the divorce.

The second common failure is naming the estate as beneficiary. When a retirement account passes to the estate, it loses its ability to use the stretch provisions available to individual beneficiaries, it becomes subject to estate creditors, and it goes through probate — the exact outcome that most estate plans are designed to avoid.

The third common failure is outdated primary beneficiaries — a deceased parent or sibling who was named decades ago and never updated. When a named beneficiary predeceases the account holder with no contingent beneficiary named, the account passes to the estate by default.

The fourth common failure is no coordination with the trust. Many estate plans are designed around a revocable trust as the primary vehicle, but the retirement accounts and life insurance were never retitled or their beneficiary designations updated to coordinate with the trust structure. The result is two separate inheritance paths that produce unintended outcomes.

## The Interaction With Creditor Planning

From a creditor protection perspective, beneficiary designations interact with the structural plan in specific ways that deserve attention.

In Florida, life insurance cash value and death benefits are exempt from creditor claims under Fla. Stat. §222.13 when paid to a named beneficiary other than the estate. If a life insurance policy names the estate as beneficiary instead of an individual, that creditor protection disappears — the proceeds become estate assets subject to creditor claims.

ERISA-qualified retirement plans — 401(k)s, pensions, profit-sharing plans — enjoy extremely strong creditor protection by federal statute. IRAs have state-law creditor protection in Florida under Fla. Stat. §222.21, though with some limitations. When these accounts pass to individual named beneficiaries, the protection framework applicable to the account applies. When they pass to the estate, the protection ends.

## What a Review Looks Like

A complete beneficiary designation review takes roughly thirty minutes and involves three steps. First, collect the current designation forms on file for every retirement account and life insurance policy you own. Your plan administrator and insurer are required to provide these on request.

Second, compare them against your current estate plan documents and your current intentions — accounting for changes in marital status, deaths of named beneficiaries, and changes in how you want assets distributed.

Third, update any designations that are inconsistent. The update forms are typically simple documents that the financial institution or insurer provides.

The diagnostic tool on this site includes a question about beneficiary designation review history. If findings surface a concern, a review coordinated with your estate planning attorney is the appropriate next step — so that the designations align with the broader plan rather than working against it.
